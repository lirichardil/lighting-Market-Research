## 1. Product Availability — Discontinuations, Backorders, Long Lead Times

### Discontinued Products Discovered After Ordering

**Katrina Tate** (Houzz forum user) documented a compounding discontinuation problem:

> "We settled on a light fixture and learned that it was discontinued after we placed the order and paid for it."

Then, after finding a replacement:

> "We found a new light fixture and just learned today that it was also discontinued in the color we prefer although it's part of the summer 2016 catalog."

And crucially:

> "Both times, I learned they were discontinued when calling to check the status of my order."

### Discontinued Mid-Phase Projects (Commercial Scale)

On what happens when manufacturers end-of-life a controls platform mid-project:

> "For most Audacy customers with completed projects, this likely won't be an issue, but for customers who just completed Phase I of a multi-phase project, it's a major disappointment and potential headache."

**Greg Ehrich**, Owner of Premier Lighting (distributor), who had spec'd Audacy for a multi-venue Minnesota sports facility expanding to a Phase II:

> "Ideal is huge. Cree Lighting, big company... Audacy, they're someone that you trust to be around."

(The trust was misplaced. The discontinuation left Phase II expansion stranded.)

Source: Inside.lighting — "The Real Impact of Discontinued Lighting Products" (June 2021) — https://inside.lighting/news/21-06/real-impact-discontinued-lighting-products

---

Industry summary of the tiered impact of discontinuations:

> "If discontinuation impacts marquis custom fixtures, niche fixtures or unique lighting controls platforms, project stakeholders are forced to engage in patchwork solutions, inferior workarounds, costly replacements or expensive upgrades. These situations can sometimes leave specifiers, distributors, contractors and end users extremely frustrated."

> "Sales cycles for controls systems can be very long, often taking 9-12 months or longer for larger commercial controls systems to flow through the various stages of design and construction."

Source: Inside.lighting — same article above

## 2. Client Education / Clients Don't Understand Lighting Until Too Late

### Clients Cannot Read a Lighting Plan

**Matthew Tirschwell**, Tirschwell & Co. (New York lighting designer):

> "Show them a lighting plan, and clients don't know how to read it."

His firm resorts to James Turrell–style mini-builds and scale models to communicate design intent in real light rather than drawings.

## 4. Specification Changes & Substitutions Mid-Project

### The "Why Bother" Fatigue

**Donna Leban, AIA, LC**, architect/lighting designer (Light/Space/Design):

> "Why bother spending so much time to select lighting when we never end up with the fixtures we specified?"

Leban wrote this as the representative frustration architects express when electrical contractors routinely substitute their carefully chosen lighting specifications during construction through value engineering.

Source: AIA Vermont — "If Value Engineering Isn't Providing the Lighting Results you Want, Try a Different Approach" — https://www.aiavt.org/news-events/news-details/post/if-value-engineering-isnt-providing-the-lighting-results-you-want-try-a-different-approach

---

### What Happens When the Substitute Is Technically Wrong

From Regency Supply and AIA sources, a concrete substitution failure scenario:

A warm color temperature specification (3000K) was substituted with a daylight fixture (5000K) with lower color rendering, making floor finishes appear dull and the space feel wrong. The architects did not catch this because they were not involved in submittal review.

> "Lighting designers, landscape architects, and designers are frustrated at how often their specified light fittings have been 'value engineered' out of their projects and replaced with cheap lookalikes that deteriorate and damage reputation and project quality."

Source: Regency Supply — "How to do value engineering the right way" — https://insights.regencysupply.com/how-to-do-value-engineering-the-right-way

---

### A $0.50 Substitution That Damaged a Mahogany Conference Table

**Avi Mor** (IALD, CLD), lighting designer and founder of Morlights:

Accepted by an architect without review, a minor fixture substitution — a cheaper trim piece — resulted in the trim falling out of the ceiling and landing on a client's mahogany conference table, causing damage.

Source: Lighting Matters Podcast — "Episode 18: What Happens When Architects Ignore Lighting Design?" — https://lightingmatterspodcast.com/episodes/episode-18-what-happens-when-architects-ignore-lighting-design

---

### Maintenance Substitutions Break Entire Schemes

From the same podcast episode, on what happens after a project is delivered:

> "Different lamps were put in, probably shouldn't have been, and fixtures have been changed out that shouldn't have been" — leading to mismatched color temperatures across identical building facades.

A school district faced a **$150,000–$200,000 replacement cost** when a manufacturer discontinued a lighting controls system, forcing a building-wide upgrade.

Source: Lighting Matters Podcast — same episode above

---

### The "Or Equal" Clause as a Bureaucratic Trap

From industry analysis on specification writing:

> "The 'or equal' clause creates significant friction, turning into 'a bureaucratic nightmare where every submittal becomes a research project' to determine equivalency."

> "In practice, it often turns into a bureaucratic nightmare where every submittal becomes a research project to determine if the proposed substitution truly meets the spec, and architects, engineers, and contractors all waste valuable time."

Source: Aorbis — "14 Specification Writing Problems No One Talks About in 2025" — https://www.aorbis.com/blog/specification-writing-problems/

---

### Specs Reference Products That No Longer Exist

From same source:

> "Specifications often list products that haven't been available for years," creating "endless back-and-forths with contractors submitting substitutions."

> "Architects repeatedly reuse old specs with minimal changes, causing 'irrelevant details being included or critical details being overlooked.'"

Source: Aorbis — same article above

### Lighting Rep Lawsuit Over Failed Custom Fixtures (2026)

Hawaii Lighting Representatives (HILR) filed suit in federal court in Arizona (May 2026) against Byzantivm Inc. (Miami) over custom chandeliers and pendants manufactured for the JW Marriott Desert Ridge Resort & Spa (Phoenix). Total purchase order: ~$620,127.

Key failures:
- **LT-08 (dichroic acrylic organic chandelier, $88,280):** Glass alleged to have a "known fail point" — pieces "detached from their metal frames and fell from the ceiling."
- **LT-09, LT-10, LT-12 (custom ballroom chandeliers, $388,540 combined):** "Clips broke the glass." Manufacturer allegedly abandoned remediation commitments.

The complaint invokes life-safety concerns twelve times. Byzantivm disputed allegations, citing improper installer assembly and late-stage design changes.

Source: Inside.lighting — "[UPDATED] Lighting Rep Sues Manufacturer Over Failed Custom Fixtures" (May 2026) — https://inside.lighting/news/26-05/lighting-rep-sues-manufacturer-over-failed-custom-fixtures

### Markel — When Value Engineering Creates Liability Exposure for Design Professionals

Value engineering (VE) substitutions can expose the original designer to legal liability when the substitute product fails — even though they didn't select it.

> "If the contractor proposes a substitution and the design professional approves it, and that substitution ultimately fails, the design professional may face claims."
